How an LLM Works:
Transformers & Attention

You already know neurons, weights, and gradient descent. An LLM is all of that — plus one new trick called attention. Type some text, watch it become numbers, click a word and see the model "pay attention," then run a real next-word generator.

1The bridge from what you know

An LLM (Large Language Model) is a giant neural network — the same neurons and weights from Part 1 — trained to do one absurdly simple thing: predict the next word.

That's genuinely it. Everything impressive an LLM does — answering questions, writing code, reasoning — emerges from getting extremely good at "what word comes next?" To do that, four problems must be solved. This page walks each one:

① Text isn't numbers → tokens & embeddings (Sections 2–3)

② Words need context from other words → attention (Section 4) ← the big new idea

③ Stack it deep → the transformer & its parameters (Sections 5–6)

④ Teach it & run it → training & generation (Sections 7–8)

2Step ① — Turning text into tokens

A neural network only does math, so text must become numbers first. But the model doesn't work word-by-word or letter-by-letter — it works in tokens: chunks that are roughly ¾ of a word. Common words are one token; rarer words get split into pieces. Type anything below and watch it tokenize.

Purple chips = a word that got split into sub-word pieces. Each token maps to an ID number in the model's fixed vocabulary (~50,000–100,000 tokens). This is why you're billed "per token" and why models have a token limit.

3Step ① cont. — Tokens become vectors (embeddings)

Each token ID is then turned into an embedding: a long list of numbers (a vector) that captures the token's meaning. The trick: words with similar meanings get similar vectors, so they sit close together in space. Real embeddings have hundreds or thousands of dimensions — impossible to draw — so here's a 2-D shadow of that idea:

Why this matters: meaning becomes geometry. "Closeness" = "similar meaning," and even relationships become directions you can do arithmetic on. This same idea (turning things into vectors and finding the nearest ones) is exactly what powers vector databases and RAG.

4Step ② — Attention ★ (the one big idea)

A word's meaning depends on the other words around it. Attention is the mechanism that lets every word look at every other word and decide which ones matter for understanding it.

Classic example: in the sentence below, what does "it" refer to — the animal or the street? A human knows instantly. The model figures it out by having "it" pay attention to the right words. Click any word to see what it attends to — the brighter a word glows, the more attention is on it.

👆 Click a word. Try “it” and “tired”.

The payoff of attention: the model builds an understanding of each word in context, looking at the whole sentence at once (not one word at a time like older models). It's also why it's fast — all words are processed in parallel — and why it handles long passages well. Doing this for every word against every other word is called self-attention, and it's the heart of the transformer.

5Step ③ — The transformer block, stacked deep

A transformer is built by stacking the same block over and over. One block = an attention layer (mix in context from other words) followed by a small neural network (the neurons from Part 1, doing per-word thinking). Stack dozens of these and you get a full LLM.

Data flows up through N identical blocks, gathering context and refining its understanding, until the top produces the next-token prediction.

6Step ③ cont. — So what ARE "the billions of parameters"?

Remember from Part 1: every connection has a weight, and weights are the learned knowledge. A transformer has weights in every attention layer and every neural-net layer, across all the stacked blocks, plus all the embedding vectors. Add them all up:

*Rule of thumb: ≈ 2 bytes per parameter. So a 70-billion-parameter model needs ≈ 140 GB just to load — which is why model size decides what hardware (and how many GPUs) you need.

Plain answer to "what's inside the billions of parameters?" Numbers. Specifically, all the attention weights, all the neuron weights and biases, and all the embedding vectors — every tunable number that gradient descent adjusted during training. More parameters = more capacity to store patterns and knowledge.

7Step ④ — How an LLM is trained (3 stages)

The weights start random (it outputs gibberish). Three stages turn it into a helpful assistant:

Stage 1 Pretraining — show it a huge chunk of the internet and have it predict the next token, trillions of times. Using the same loss + gradient descent + backprop loop from Part 1. To get good at predicting the next word, it's forced to absorb grammar, facts, reasoning, and code. Costs millions of dollars, weeks on thousands of GPUs. Result: a “base model” — smart but just an autocomplete.

Stage 2 Fine-tuning (instruction tuning) — keep training on curated “instruction → good answer” examples so it learns to follow requests instead of just continuing text.

Stage 3 RLHF (Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback) — humans rank answers best-to-worst; the model is nudged to produce answers humans prefer (more helpful, honest, harmless). This is the step that turned raw GPT-3 into ChatGPT.

8Step ④ cont. — Generating text ★ (with a temperature dial)

At inference time the model generates one token at a time: it predicts the next token, appends it, then feeds the whole thing back in to predict the next — over and over. (That's why you see answers stream out word by word.) For each step it produces a probability for every possible next token. Temperature controls how boldly it picks.

Two more terms this explains: Context window = how many tokens the model can “see” at once (prompt + answer so far) — its working memory. Hallucination = it confidently states something false, because it's predicting plausible tokens, not verified truths — there's no built-in fact-checker. (That's the gap RAG fills by feeding it real documents.)

9Putting it all together

Text is split into tokens, each turned into a meaning-vector (embedding). Those flow up through a stack of transformer blocks; in each block, attention lets every token gather context from every other token, and a small neural net thinks about it. After many blocks, the model outputs a probability for the next token, which temperature shapes into a choice. Append it, feed it back, repeat — that's generation. All the weights that make this work are the billions of parameters, set by pretraining (next-token prediction over the internet), then fine-tuning and RLHF. The whole thing runs on GPUs because it's mountains of the matrix math from Part 1.
